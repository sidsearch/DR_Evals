# The 2008 Financial Crisis: A Comprehensive Analysis of Causes and Economic Consequences

## Executive Summary
The 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) represents the most severe financial disruption since the Great Depression, originating in the US subprime mortgage market before cascading into a synchronized global recession. This report examines its multi-layered causes and quantifies its far-reaching economic consequences.

## Part I: Root Causes

### 1.1 Structural Failures in the US Housing Market
Between 2001–2006, US home prices rose ~124% nationally (Case-Shiller Index), fueled by historically low interest rates following the dot-com bust and 9/11. Lenders, incentivized by an originate-to-distribute model, relaxed underwriting standards dramatically. By 2006, roughly 20% of all new mortgages were subprime, with many featuring adjustable rates, negative amortization, and no income verification ("NINJA loans").

### 1.2 Securitization and the Shadow Banking System
Subprime mortgages were pooled into Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS), then repackaged into Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs) and synthetic CDOs. This multi-layered securitization obscured underlying risk and distributed it across the global financial system. Off-balance-sheet Special Investment Vehicles (SIVs) allowed banks to hold these assets without commensurate capital reserves, effectively circumventing Basel I capital requirements.

### 1.3 Systemic Conflicts of Interest
Credit rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) operated under an issuer-pays model, creating incentives to award AAA ratings to tranches that empirically carried much higher default risk. Internal emails later revealed analysts were aware of model limitations. Simultaneously, the repeal of Glass-Steagall (1999, Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act) allowed commercial banks to engage in investment banking activities, amplifying risk concentration.

### 1.4 Regulatory Gaps and Leverage
The SEC's 2004 Net Capital Rule change allowed major investment banks to increase leverage ratios from ~12:1 to over 30:1. Derivatives (especially credit default swaps, CDS) remained largely unregulated under the Commodity Futures Modernization Act (2000), with AIG alone writing ~$440B in CDS with insufficient collateral.

## Part II: Economic Consequences

### 2.1 Immediate Financial Shock (2008–2009)
- **US GDP**: Contracted 4.3% peak-to-trough (Q4 2007–Q2 2009), the largest post-WWII recession
- **Unemployment**: Rose from 4.7% (Nov 2007) to 10.0% (Oct 2009); U-6 underemployment peaked at 17.1%
- **Wealth destruction**: US household net worth fell ~$13 trillion in 2008 alone
- **Credit markets**: LIBOR-OIS spread spiked to 364 bps in Oct 2008, signaling acute interbank stress

### 2.2 Fiscal and Monetary Response
TARP injected $700B into the financial system; the Fed's balance sheet expanded from ~$900B to $2.3T by end of 2008 via emergency lending facilities (TALF, PDCF, MMIFF). Despite unprecedented intervention, the US lost ~8.7 million jobs, with many never recovering to pre-crisis quality.

### 2.3 Global Transmission
The crisis spread via trade finance contraction (global trade fell ~12% in 2009, the steepest post-WWII decline), cross-border bank deleveraging, and synchronized confidence collapse. The Eurozone sovereign debt crisis (2010–2012) was a direct downstream consequence, as bank bailouts ballooned government deficits in Ireland, Spain, and Portugal.

### 2.4 Long-Run Scarring Effects
Research by Reinhart & Rogoff (2009) and subsequent IMF work found that banking crises typically reduce potential output by 4–7% permanently. The US labor force participation rate declined from 66.4% (2007) to 62.7% (2015) and never fully recovered—suggesting persistent hysteresis effects beyond cyclical unemployment.

## Part III: Regulatory Reform
Dodd-Frank (2010) introduced the Volcker Rule, mandatory central clearing for derivatives, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), and systemically important financial institution (SIFI) designations with enhanced capital requirements. Basel III raised Tier 1 capital requirements and introduced liquidity coverage ratios. However, critics argue partial Dodd-Frank rollback (2018 Economic Growth Act) re-exposed mid-size banks to elevated risk.

## Conclusion
The GFC resulted from a confluence of microeconomic incentive failures (originate-to-distribute, issuer-pays ratings), macroeconomic imbalances (global savings glut, low rates), and regulatory gaps (shadow banking, derivatives). Its economic legacy—reduced potential output, increased inequality, and lasting labor market damage—underscores that financial crises carry costs far exceeding their immediate GDP impact.
